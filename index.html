I work on continual reinforcement learning and neuroscience-inspired AI. Broadly, I am interested in how artificial agents can keep learning in changing environments without becoming brittle, forgetting what they already know, or losing the ability to adapt.

My current work focuses on architectural mechanisms for preserving plasticity in neural networks. Instead of only asking how to train agents better, I ask whether we can design networks that are structurally better suited for long-term adaptation.

About me

I am an integrated MS/PhD student at the Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology, advised by Prof. Sang Wan Lee. Before coming to KAIST, I studied Artificial Intelligence at Radboud University in the Netherlands and spent an exchange semester in Applied Artificial Intelligence at Sungkyunkwan University.

I like research problems that sit between algorithm design, learning dynamics, and cognitive science. Many of the questions I care about come from a simple intuition: biological learning systems are rarely trained once and then frozen. They keep adapting, but usually without completely destroying what was useful before. I want to understand how to bring some of that robustness into artificial agents.

Research interests

My general research interests include reinforcement learning, continual learning, computational neuroscience, and adaptive neural architectures. I am particularly interested in:

  • Continual reinforcement learning: agents that remain useful under task shifts, environmental change, and long training horizons.
  • Architectural plasticity: neural network layers and mechanisms that preserve adaptability as part of the model design, not only through extra losses or replay buffers.
  • Dynamic computation: systems that learn when to spend computation and when to skip, reuse, or compress internal processing.
  • Neuroscience-inspired AI: using ideas from neural dynamics and cognitive control to build more robust learning systems.

A recurring theme in my work is the stability-plasticity trade-off: how can an agent preserve useful structure while still changing when the world changes?

Selected projects

Interpolation Layers

Interpolation Layers are a simple drop-in replacement for standard neural network layers that help networks maintain adaptability in continual learning settings by interpolating between a stable pathway and a learnable pathway.

Dynamic time perception for reinforcement learning

This project studies whether RL agents can save computation by dynamically skipping internal updates when the environment or hidden state changes slowly. The goal is not just efficiency, but a more cognitive view of when an agent needs detailed temporal processing.
